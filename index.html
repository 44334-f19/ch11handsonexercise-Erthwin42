Automatic Copyrights

All original works created and in some way made tangible is automatically protected under U.S. law. This includes published and unpublished works. Copyright law protects literary, dramatic, musical, and artistic works. This includes computer software, such as files, websites, and images. Copyright registration is not required but can be done to have facts of the copyright on public record, certificate of registration, and for legal protection reasons. Owners of registered works are entitled to statutory damages for copyright infringement and attorney's fees in successful litigation. U.S. Copyright is honored in many, but not all other countries, by default.

Fair Use Law

Some portions of works may be used without approval, provided they fall under fair use. This includes uses for the advancement of public interest, such as education, especially when the amount of copied work is small and the original author/artist is given appropriate credit. Some uses may be considered approved by implication, such as public e-mail lists, or possibly forum posts, where forwarding and archiving is considered to be pre-approved unless the author expressly forbids it. Usage that generates income or otherwise interferes with the original owner's income, is not considered fair use. The overall design, links, original text, graphics, audio, and video, along with other unique and original elements, of a website are covered by copyright law. Internet users cannot transfer or copy from a site and post them without permission from the owner without breaking copyright law. If you are unsure of whether something is Fair Use or not it is advised to request permission to use or not to use the work at all.

Specific Laws

N.E.T. (No Electronic Theft) prevents the creation and distribution of unauthorized copies of copyrighted works without profit motive. When products worth $1000 or more are copied, regardless of sale or being given away, the reproducer may be found guilty of cpyright infringement under the N.E.T. Act. For 1 or more copies their is a fine and up to one year jail time. For 10 or more works, with a value of more than $2500, there is a fine or up to three years jail time, with repeat offenses increasing jail time to up to six years. The N.E.T. act also defines financial gain as it relates to financial gain, expanding it to mean collection, or expected collection of anything of value, including other copyrighted works.

The Digital Millenium Copyright Act protects against technological measures meant to block access to copyrighted works and distribution of false information about a work. This makes it illegal to provide false copyright information, distribute or import for distribution false copyright information, remove or alter copyright information, and distributing or importing works known to have alterations of copyright information. Penalties include both civil and criminal penalties.
